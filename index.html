Abstract

Music style transfer enables personalized music creation by blending the structure of a source with the stylistic attributes of a reference. While existing approaches have shown promise, they often struggle to faithfully capture the fine-grained nuances of audio exemplars under a zero-shot setting, predominantly relying on coarse text descriptions or requiring computationally expensive task-specific training. We present \textbf{Stylus}, a \textit{training-free} framework that reformulates music style transfer as a representation-level manipulation of Mel-spectrograms, interpreted as structured time--frequency images. By repurposing pretrained image diffusion models, Stylus leverages visual spatial priors to disentangle musical structure (geometry) from timbre (texture). This is achieved via an attention-based inductive bias where content queries anchor the musical layout while style keys and values carry stylistic textures. To ensure high fidelity, we introduce a phase-preserving reconstruction strategy that prioritizes structural stability and eliminates the metallic artifacts common in iterative phase estimation. Extensive evaluations against state-of-the-art baselines demonstrate that Stylus achieves a superior balance of content preservation and stylistic fit. Quantitative metrics, including FAD, KLD, and Chroma, and a user study with 2,925 ratings confirm that Stylus significantly outperforms existing methods ($p < 0.05$), achieving 34.1\% higher content preservation and 25.7\% better perceptual quality without any additional training.

Stylus transfers the timbral style of a reference audio onto a content track — entirely training-free — by repurposing Stable Diffusion on mel-spectrograms.

User Study

We conducted a perceptual evaluation with 2,925 ratings from human listeners comparing Stylus against MusicGen-melody and MusicTI across two axes: content preservation and perceptual quality. Stylus significantly outperforms all baselines (p < 0.05), achieving 34.1% higher content preservation and 25.7% better perceptual quality.
